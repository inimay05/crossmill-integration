The Novel Idea

What if they could learn from each other?

Despite appearances, orange juice processing and blast furnace steelmaking share deep structural symmetry — the same underlying physics, the same optimisation trade-offs, just different scales.

"Structural isomorphism: different industries, identical control problems. A policy that learns to manage thermal ramps in a pasteuriser already knows something about managing temperature in a furnace."

Shared memory across process boundaries

Anti-Reward-Hacking

Flags that keep the agent honest

Industrial RL policies are uniquely dangerous: a reward-hacking agent in a food factory or steel plant can cause regulatory violations, equipment destruction, or public health incidents. CrossMill ships five structured penalty flags.

SAFETY_WARNING Triggered when the agent approaches but has not yet violated a safety constraint — early warning system for unsafe trajectories.
CATASTROPHIC_FAIL Hard safety constraint violated — FDA pathogen exposure, thermal runaway, or refractory breach. Immediate large penalty + episode termination.
QUALITY_LOW Output quality falls below threshold while safety constraints are technically satisfied — detects reward gaming through quality sacrifice.
HIGH_VARIANCE Control actions exhibit implausibly high variance, characteristic of exploitation-mode policy collapse or oscillation attacks.
COMPOUND_FAIL Multiple flags co-occur within a short window — compounded penalty captures correlated constraint violations that individual flags miss.
Transfer Gain Formula
transfer_gain = (R_cross − R_none)  /  |R_none|
where   R_cross = reward with cross-industry memory,   R_none = baseline reward (no memory)
The primary evaluation metric — positive transfer_gain proves that cross-industry knowledge genuinely helps, not just that more training helps.
